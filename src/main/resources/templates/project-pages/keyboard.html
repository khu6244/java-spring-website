Custom Mechanical Keyboard

This page explains how I began my keyboard build, documents the process, and essentially serves as a way of expressing my pride in a passion project I have wanted to do for a long time and was ecstatic with when I finished.

I bought my first mechanical keyboard in Grade 9, and I loved it. The tactile and auditory feedback, deep travel, and aesthetics of mechanical keyboard resonated with me from the moment I started typing. In high school, I had to type a lot. I wrote almost every essay, assignment, and project on this clunky, oversized keyboard that was absurdly loud. I didn't even consider that there were better options out there, but that changed in my first year of university.
In first year, I decided that I wanted to learn how to solder and learn how to work with Arduinos. At some point, I decided that building a custom Arduino-powered mechanical keyboard would be the perfect opportunity to try both of those in the same project. I browsed through various online guides and posts, and eventually settled on the form factor found in the beginner's guide on 68keys.io. The keyboard itself is based on the Magicforce 68 consumer keyboard, a popular affordable starting point for keyboard modding. While I followed the guide for most of the build, I added on where I could and had to diverge slightly to work around some issues.

The Build

Lasercut Plates and PCB
I began on the 68keys.io website by getting blank keyboard top and bottom plate CAD drawings, autogenerated from a website that does so based on a chosen keyboard layout. I needed these CAD drawings to lasercut the aluminum plates, but I wanted to laser etch the plates to make them personalized. I ended up using AutoCAD to draw my name in both English and Chinese on the front plate of the keyboard above the arrow keys. I had very little experience in PCB design at this point, having barely scratched the surface working in Altium Designer for RSX, the design team that I am a part of. Instead of designing my own, I decided to just use the one provided by the guide, as it was an established, reputable design that I knew would work and had all the features I wanted plus some I didn't even consider.
Electrical Components, Switches, and Keycaps
The next step was to decide on and buy keyboard switches, keycaps, various electrical components, and hardware. I bought an Arduino Pro Micro and a long list of assorted electrical parts from Digikey, mostly consisting of diodes (important in a mechanical keyboard to faciliate n-key rollover), resistors, LEDs, a micro-USB to micro-USB extension cord, as well as some hardware (M5 screws, standoffs, and some rubber bumpers). These are fairly standard components, and the interesting part comes with the keycaps and switch selections. My previous keyboard contained Cherry MX-Blue switches, which were mediocre as far as mechanical switches go. However, I really enjoy both the tactile and auditory feedback, so I opted for clicky switches as opposed to linear or tactile. I type extremely heavily, so I opted for switches with the strongest springs I could find - NovelKeys x Kailh Speed Navy Thick Clicks, which have about 100g of activation force. For comparison, most common switches activate with about 60-70g of force, so these are fairly heavy. I knew that I wanted blank keycaps because I can touch type and I would personally find them much more aesthetically pleasing, but blank keycaps are somewhat uncommon and I did not have many choices. I ended up opting for two sets of sky blue and pink blank keycaps on AliExpress, and mixing the two for a two-tone look. I opted to use blue for the alpha keys, with the exception of pink home row indicators. However, the keyboard layout is actually set up for a variant of Colemak that I am learning, which is why it makes no sense for QWERTY.
